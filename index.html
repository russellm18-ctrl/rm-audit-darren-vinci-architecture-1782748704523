// Findings

  • THE STRUCTURAL METADATA COLLAPSE: Your project images—the Brier New SFR exterior, the Shoreline 8-unit townhouse elevations, the Bellevue Courtyard House courtyard renders—are being indexed as anonymous visual files with no alt-text, no JSON-LD schema tying them to specific project types, locations, or services, and almost certainly no embedded EXIF metadata linking them to the Greater Seattle market. A high-intent buyer right now searching "custom home architect Bellevue WA" or "middle housing infill architect Shoreline Washington" is landing on a competitor whose images carry structured geographic and service-type signals yours do not. You are invisible to that query despite having a delivered project in Shoreline and an active build in Bellevue's 98006 zip code.
  • THE HIDDEN TRUST DEFICIT: The high-net-worth custom home client considering a $4,500–$4,700 sq ft residence—exactly your Brier and Bellevue Courtyard client profile—needs photographic proof of completed construction before they commit a six-figure design fee to a boutique single-principal firm. Your Brier project completed April 2025, yet the site shows no finished photography, only written descriptions of the double-height atrium, the 6–8 foot overhang detail, and the matte black metal trim—design specifics that would photograph as compelling proof of craft. Written claims about "contemporary elegance" and "luxurious yet comfortable" without a single post-construction image are a trust void a larger firm with a full portfolio fills immediately.
  • THE COMPETITIVE EXPOSURE: Established Seattle-area residential firms—including the very lineage you reference, Vandeventer + Carlander and Olson Walker—maintain deep visual libraries of completed projects shot at multiple construction phases: framing, envelope, finish, and occupancy. A prospective Eastside developer evaluating your Shoreline 8-unit townhouse work against a competitor with documented infill project photography, material palette callouts, and unit subdivision diagrams will route the contract away from you, representing a multi-unit development fee that likely starts at $150,000–$250,000. Your material strategy—the layered Hardie, dark brown metal cladding, vertical wood siding—is genuinely differentiated on paper but completely invisible as a competitive asset because no finished imagery exists to demonstrate it.
  • THE PHOTO-FIRST UPGRADE: Commission a structured two-phase shoot: Phase 1 targets the completed Brier residence immediately—interior sequences prioritizing the double-height atrium, the overhang-to-massing junction, and the three-car garage volume, plus full exterior elevations tied to seasonal Pacific Northwest context. Phase 2 documents the Vuecrest and Bellevue Courtyard projects through construction milestones, creating a phased asset library rather than a single end-state dump. Every image file delivered must carry embedded metadata—project type, city, zip code, architect credit, material callouts—and be deployed site-side with JSON-LD Project schema, geo-tagged alt-text referencing specific neighborhoods (Brier, Shoreline, Bellevue 98006), and structured image sitemaps. This converts your existing completed work from a written case study into a search-indexed, trust-building visual archive that closes the gap between your demonstrated craft and your current market invisibility.
